Scanner

E-Waste Electronics

Material Composition

Lead: used in solder on circuit boards. Toxic
Mercury: may be found in older fluorescent backlights or switches. Toxic
Cadmium: sometimes present in circuit boards or batteries.Toxic
Chromium: used in metal coatings or components. Toxic
Aluminum: used in structural parts and casing.Safe
Steel: found in mechanical and support components.Safe
Copper: used in wiring and circuits.Safe

Why Proper Disposal Matters

Environmental Damage

Toxic metals can leach into soil and groundwater if disposed of in landfills. Mercury and cadmium can contaminate water systems, harming aquatic life. Improper disposal contributes to overall electronic waste pollution.

Resource Waste

Scanners contain valuable metals like copper and aluminum that can be recovered and reused. Recycling reduces the need for mining new raw materials. Keeps non-renewable resources in use and reduces landfill volume.

Health Risks

Lead, mercury, and cadmium are harmful to humans if inhaled or ingested. Exposure can cause neurological, kidney, and respiratory problems. Workers and nearby communities are at risk from unsafe e-waste handling.

How and Where to Dispose

Electronics recycling

Do NOT dispose of scanners in household trash.Dispose at:

  • Authorized e-waste recycling centers
  • Manufacturer take-back programs (e.g., HP, Canon, Epson)
  • Retailer collection programs (Best Buy, Staples, etc.)
  • Local government e-waste collection events
  • Ensure devices are intact and not broken to avoid toxic material release.
  • Remove any batteries before disposal.

Additional information
  • Donating working scanners can extend their life and reduce waste.
  • Scanners are subject to e-waste regulations in many countries.
  • Public education about safe disposal improves compliance.
  • Some parts (like glass plates) can also be recycled.
  • Choosing energy-efficient or multi-function devices reduces overall e-waste.
  • Proper disposal supports a circular economy and reduces environmental impact.
